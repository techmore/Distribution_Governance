# Operating Agreement: Key Provisions

The Operating Agreement is the governing document for an LLC. It overrides the default "default rules" of the Pennsylvania Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (15 Pa.C.S. Ch. 88) and establishes the bespoke arrangement the four members have negotiated. Below are the provisions most relevant to this venture.

## 1. Capital Contributions

Each member's initial capital contribution should be documented in a **Schedule A** attached to the agreement. The schedule should specify:

- Cash amount contributed (or agreed future contribution timeline)
- Valuation of any non-cash contributions (equipment, IP, existing relationships)
- Whether additional capital calls require unanimous or majority consent
- Consequences for failing to meet a capital call (dilution, conversion to debt, etc.)

> **PA Law Note:** Under 15 Pa.C.S. § 8842, a member's obligation to contribute capital is enforceable only if set forth in the Operating Agreement.

## 2. Profit, Loss & Distributions

How the money flows is the most negotiated section. Consider a **waterfall distribution** structure:

| Tier | Distribution | Recipient |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Return of capital | Members pro rata to their capital accounts |
| 2 | Preferred return (e.g., 8%) | Members with capital invested |
| 3 | Promote split | Operating members (60%) / Capital member (40%) |
| 4 | Residual | Pro rata to ownership % |

Alternatively, a **pro-rata distribution** based solely on ownership percentages is simpler but may not fairly reward operating members who contribute time without equal capital.

### Tax Considerations
- The LLC will file Form 1065 (Partnership Return) and issue Schedule K-1s to each member
- Consider a **target allocation** provision to ensure tax distributions track economic distributions
- Section 704(b) requires that tax allocations have "substantial economic effect"

## 3. Management & Voting

### Manager-Managed Structure
- One or more members are designated as **Managers** with authority over day-to-day operations
- **Member Votes** are reserved for major decisions

### Voting Thresholds

| Action | Vote Required |
|--------|---------------|
| Ordinary business decisions | Manager discretion or majority |
| Admission of new members | Unanimous |
| Sale of substantially all assets | Unanimous |
| Incurring debt above $X | Supermajority (75%) |
| Amendment to Operating Agreement | Unanimous |
| Dissolution | Unanimous |

### Deadlock Resolution
Consider one of these mechanisms:
- **Mediation** before litigation
- **Texas shoot-out** — one member names a price; the other must either buy at that price or sell at that price
- **Outside expert** — a neutral third party resolves the deadlock

## 4. Transfer Restrictions

In a closely-held LLC, you generally want to restrict who can become a member:

- **Right of first refusal** — Members must offer interests to the company/other members first
- **Approval requirement** — Transfers to outsiders require unanimous consent
- **Permitted transfers** — Usually allowed to family members or trusts without triggering ROFR
- **Tag-along / drag-along** — Protects minority members (tag-along) and allows majority to force sale (drag-along)

## 5. Buy-Sell Provisions

Also called a **buyout** clause. Common triggering events:

- **Death or disability** — Company has option (or obligation) to purchase the member's interest
- **Voluntary withdrawal** — Member wants to leave; company buys them out at fair value
- **Termination for cause** — Breach, competition, criminal activity; potentially at a discount
- **Valuation method** — Agreed formula (e.g., multiple of trailing EBITDA), appraisal, or "agreed value" updated annually

## 6. Non-Compete & Confidentiality

Given that all four members run existing businesses, the non-compete must be carefully scoped:

- **Limited scope** — Only restrict competition with the venture's specific business (distribution of [category] products in PA/NJ)
- **Limited duration** — During membership and 1-2 years after
- **Carve-outs** — Members' existing businesses should be explicitly excluded
- **Confidentiality** — Protect customer lists, supplier terms, pricing, and financial data

## 7. Dissolution & Winding Up

Define what happens if the venture fails or members agree to shut down:

- **Voluntary dissolution** — Requires unanimous consent
- **Involuntary dissolution** — Triggered by bankruptcy, illegality, or court order
- **Winding up** — Appoint a liquidating manager; sell assets; pay creditors; distribute remainder per the waterfall
- **Termination** — File Articles of Cancellation with PA Department of State
