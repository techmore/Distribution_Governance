# Pennsylvania &amp; New Jersey Marijuana Regulatory Context

Understanding the regulatory environment is critical for any distribution business touching cannabis-related products. Even if the venture distributes non-plant-touching ancillary products, the proximity to the industry carries compliance implications.

## Pennsylvania — Medical Marijuana Program

**Governing Law:** Pennsylvania Medical Marijuana Act (Act 16 of 2016)

**Regulator:** Pennsylvania Department of Health — Office of Medical Marijuana

### License Types
- **Grower/Processor** — Cultivation and processing
- **Dispensary** — Retail sale to patients
- **Clinical Registrant** — Combined growing/dispensing for health systems
- **Laboratory** — Testing

### Key Facts
- Medical use only (as of 2025); adult-use legislation is pending but not yet enacted
- Patients must register with the DOH and obtain a certification from an approved physician
- Dispensaries are vertically integrated with grower/processors in many cases
- Advertising restrictions apply — no marketing that appeals to children or makes unsubstantiated claims

### Distribution-Specific Considerations
- The PA program does not have a separate "distributor" license type (unlike CA or MA)
- Third-party logistics and distribution services should be structured as service agreements, not product sales, to avoid triggering licensure requirements
- Transportation must comply with DOH security and tracking requirements

## New Jersey — Medical & Adult-Use

**Governing Law:**
- Jake Honig Compassionate Use Medical Cannabis Act (medical)
- Cannabis Regulatory, Enforcement Assistance, and Marketplace Modernization Act — **CREAMMA** (adult-use, enacted 2021)

**Regulator:** New Jersey Cannabis Regulatory Commission (CRC)

### License Types (Adult-Use)
| Class | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Class 1 | Cultivator |
| Class 2 | Manufacturer |
| Class 3 | Wholesaler |
| Class 4 | Distributor |
| Class 5 | Retailer |
| Class 6 | Delivery Service |
| Class 7 | Consumption Area |

### Key Facts
- Adult-use (recreational) sales began April 2022
- NJ has a specific **Distributor license (Class 4)** — this is the most relevant classification for this venture
- Conditional licenses available for social equity applicants
- Municipal opt-out is allowed — towns can prohibit cannabis businesses
- No vertical integration required (unlike PA's model)

### Distribution-Specific Considerations
- A **Class 4 Distributor license** authorizes the transport and delivery of cannabis items between licensed businesses
- Distributors may also provide storage, logistics, and transportation services
- The CRC requires comprehensive security, inventory tracking, and record-keeping
- License cap and market saturation are ongoing policy discussions

## Cross-State Considerations

| Factor | PA | NJ |
|--------|----|----|
| **Adult-use** | No (pending legislation) | Yes (since 2022) |
| **Medical** | Yes | Yes |
| **Separate distributor license** | No | Yes (Class 4) |
| **Vertical integration** | Encouraged | Not required |
| **Interstate transport** | Prohibited (federal law) | Prohibited (federal law) |
| **Tax rate** | None on medical | 6.625% state sales tax + municipal (up to 2%) |
| **License cap** | Yes | Yes (under review) |

## Federal Landscape & Banking

### Section 280E of the Internal Revenue Code
Businesses that traffic in Schedule I or II controlled substances cannot deduct ordinary business expenses. This applies to plant-touching cannabis businesses. **However**, businesses that only provide ancillary services (consulting, logistics, software, etc.) may not be subject to 280E — a critical distinction for entity structuring and tax planning.

### SAFE Banking Act
The SAFE Banking Act (or subsequent versions like SAFER Banking Act) would allow cannabis businesses access to traditional banking services. As of 2025, federal reform has not passed, but momentum continues. Structure your entity and operations to be **bank-friendly** from day one:
- Maintain separate accounts for each licensed entity
- Implement robust compliance and reporting
- Avoid any direct handling of cannabis or cannabis-derived revenue in non-licensed entities

## Practical Considerations for This Venture

1. **Entity structure should separate risk** — Consider holding the PA and NJ operations in separate LLCs under a single holding company to isolate regulatory risk
2. **Non-plant-touching vs. plant-touching** — If the venture only distributes branded accessories, packaging, or non-infused products, the regulatory burden is lower but not zero
3. **Attorney General guidance** — The 2013 Cole Memo (federal enforcement discretion) was rescinded in 2018. Current enforcement is limited but unpredictable
4. **Insurance** — Standard business insurance may exclude cannabis-related activities. Specialized cannabis insurance is available and recommended
